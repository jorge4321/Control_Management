Corporate Strategy

Managing the Multi-Business Firm

What is Corporate Strategy?

While Business Strategy asks "How do we compete in this market?", Corporate Strategy asks:


"What businesses should we be in?"

It is about managing a portfolio of businesses to create value that sums to more than the individual parts.

Three Key Decisions

1. Directional Strategy

Should we grow, stabilize, or retrench? (Growth vectors, M&A, exits).

2. Portfolio Analysis

Which units get resources? Which ones provide cash? (BCG Matrix).

3. Parenting Strategy

How does the headquarters add value to the individual business units? (Synergies).

Growth Vectors (Ansoff Revisited)

From a corporate perspective, growth drives valuation.

Concentration

Focusing on the current industry (Vertical/Horizontal integration).

Diversification

Moving into new industries (Related or Unrelated).

Types of Diversification

Related Diversification

Entering a new business that links to existing activities.

Ex: Disney buying Pixar (both animation).

Goal: Synergy

Unrelated Diversification

Entering a completely different business.

Ex: Berkshire Hathaway (Insurance, Candy, Trains).

Goal: Financial Risk Spreading

Vertical Integration

Moving up or down the supply chain.

Raw Materials
Manufacturer (You)
Retail / Consumer

Backward Integration

Buying suppliers (e.g., Netflix making its own content).

Forward Integration

Buying distributors (e.g., Apple opening Apple Stores).

Horizontal Integration

Acquiring Competitors

Buying a company at the same level of the value chain in the same industry.


  • Reduces competition.
  • Increases market share.
  • Creates economies of scale.

Risk: Antitrust regulation (Monopoly laws).

Why do M&A?

M&A is a primary tool of Corporate Strategy.

Speed

Faster to buy technology/brand than to build it from scratch.

Market Power

Remove a rival and gain pricing power.

Overcoming Barriers

Bypassing entry barriers in new markets or countries.

The M&A Reality Check

70-90%

Of acquisitions fail to deliver the promised value.


Why do they fail?

  • Culture Clash: "The way we do things here" doesn't match.
  • Overpayment: The "Winner's Curse" (Paying too much premium).
  • Integration Issues: Systems and teams don't merge smoothly.

Strategic Alliances & JVs

Sometimes, you don't need to buy the whole company.

Joint Venture (JV)

Two firms create a third independent entity.

High commitment, shared risk.

Non-Equity Alliance

Contractual agreement to supply, produce, or distribute.

Flexible, easier to exit.

Portfolio Management

Top management must view the corporation as a portfolio of investments.


The Goal: Allocate capital (money) to the units that will generate the highest return or strategic value.

We use tools like matrices to visualize this.

The BCG Matrix

Stars

High Growth, High Share. Invest.

Question Marks

High Growth, Low Share. Decide.

Cash Cows

Low Growth, High Share. Milk it.

Dogs

Low Growth, Low Share. Divest.

Boston Consulting Group

Classifies business units based on:

  1. Market Growth Rate
  2. Relative Market Share

Use cash from Cows to fund Stars.

The GE-McKinsey Matrix

A more complex 9-box matrix (3x3).


Y-Axis: Industry Attractiveness

Market size, profitability, trends, PESTEL factors.

X-Axis: Business Unit Strength

Market share, brand strength, profit margins.

Strategy: Grow/Build, Hold, or Harvest/Exit.

The Parenting Advantage

Why have a Headquarters?

If the HQ doesn't add value, the businesses would be better off independent (or investors could just diversify their own stock portfolio).


HQ must add value through:

  • Shared Services (HR, IT, Legal).
  • Brand reputation.
  • Access to cheaper capital.

Global Strategy

Expanding beyond domestic borders.

Global Strategy

Standardized products. One world, one market. (High Efficiency, Low Local Responsiveness).

Multi-domestic

Tailored products for each country. (Low Efficiency, High Local Responsiveness).

Transnational

The "Holy Grail": High Efficiency AND High Local Responsiveness. (Difficult to achieve).

International Entry Modes

Low Risk / Low Control High Risk / High Control

Exporting

Selling domestic goods abroad.

Licensing / Franchising

Letting locals use your brand for a fee.

Joint Venture

Sharing ownership with a local partner.

Wholly Owned Subsidiary

Building your own factory/office (FDI).

Synergy: The Magic Word

1 + 1 = 3

Synergy exists when the interaction of two business units creates value greater than the sum of their individual effects.

Cost Synergy

Sharing warehouses, IT systems, or sales teams to cut costs.

Revenue Synergy

Cross-selling products (e.g., selling insurance to bank customers).

Restructuring: Knowing When to Sell

Retrenchment Strategies

Sometimes corporate strategy is about shrinking, not growing.


  • Divestiture: Selling a business unit (Spin-off).
  • Liquidation: Closing down and selling assets.
  • Turnaround: Drastic cost-cutting to save a failing unit.

Governance & ESG

Corporate Strategy is overseen by the Board of Directors.

Environmental

Climate change strategy, carbon footprint, waste management.

Social

Labor standards, diversity, community impact.

Governance

Board structure, executive pay, anti-corruption.

Modern strategy must account for all stakeholders, not just shareholders.

Conclusion

Corporate Strategy is the high-level game of chess. It determines the shape of the firm and how it allocates its limited resources to win in multiple arenas.
