Incentive Alignment

Designing Compensation to Drive Strategy

The Agency Problem

Principal vs. Agent

In modern corporations, the owners (Shareholders) do not run the company. They hire managers (Agents) to do it.


  • Shareholders want long-term value and dividends.
  • Managers might want big offices, private jets, or short-term bonuses.

Goal: Align the interests of both parties.

Why Alignment Matters

A well-designed strategy is useless if the incentives encourage different behavior.

Direction

Incentives signal to employees what is truly important to the company.

Motivation

They provide the "fuel" for discretionary effort.

Retention

They attract and keep high-performers while filtering out underperformers.

Motivation: Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic

Extrinsic (The Carrot)

Driven by external rewards.

  • Salaries
  • Bonuses
  • Promotions

Good for mechanical tasks.

Intrinsic (The Drive)

Driven by internal satisfaction.

  • Autonomy: Desire to direct our own lives.
  • Mastery: Urge to get better.
  • Purpose: Serving something larger.

Essential for creative/strategic work (Daniel Pink).

Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory

Compensation is often a "Hygiene Factor".


  • Hygiene Factors: (Salary, Job Security). If missing, they cause dissatisfaction. But increasing them does NOT increase motivation indefinitely.
  • Motivators: (Recognition, Achievement). These actually drive higher performance.

The Formula for Motivation

Expectancy Theory (Victor Vroom)

For an incentive to work, three things must be true:

Expectancy

"If I try, can I do it?"

x

Instrumentality

"If I do it, will I get the reward?"

x

Valence

"Do I actually want the reward?"

If any of these is zero, Motivation is zero.

Linking Strategy to Pay

The BSC Connection

The Balanced Scorecard is the perfect tool to determine variable pay.


Instead of basing bonuses only on Profit (Financial), utilize a weighted average of the 4 perspectives.

Example Bonus Formula:

  • 40% Financial Targets
  • 20% Customer Net Promoter Score
  • 20% Process Efficiency
  • 20% Employee Training

The "Line of Sight" Problem

An incentive only works if the employee feels they can influence the outcome.


CEO / Executives

High Line of Sight to Stock Price & Global Profit.

Incentive: Stock Options

Factory Worker

Low Line of Sight to Stock Price.

High Line of Sight to Daily Output & Quality.

Incentive: Shift Bonus

Short-Term vs. Long-Term

Balancing today's bread with tomorrow's harvest.

Short-Term (STI)

< 1 Year

Annual Bonus, Sales Commissions.

Focus: Execution, Cash Flow.

Long-Term (LTI)

3-5 Years

Stock Options, Restricted Stock Units (RSUs).

Focus: Sustainability, Value Creation.

The Risk

Too much STI encourages cutting corners (e.g., stopping R&D to boost quarterly profit).

Individual vs. Team Incentives

Individual Pay

Rewards personal effort. Good for sales ("eat what you kill").

Risk: Can destroy teamwork and create silos.


Team Pay

Rewards collective success. Encourages collaboration.

Risk: The "Free Rider" problem (lazy members get paid).

Executive Compensation

A highly scrutinized area of corporate governance.

Stock Options

Right to buy shares at a fixed price. Valuable only if stock goes UP.

Vesting Periods

"Golden Handcuffs". You must stay 3-4 years to get the money.

Clawbacks

The right to take money back if the executive committed fraud or ethical violations.

It's Not Just About Money

Selecting the Right Metrics

Be careful what you wish for. You will get it.


Bad Metric:

"Call Center Average Handling Time (AHT)"

Result: Agents hang up on customers to keep calls short.

Better Metric:

"First Call Resolution (FCR)"

Result: Agents take time to solve the problem so the customer doesn't call back.

Gaming the System: The Cobra Effect

The Story

British India offered a bounty for every dead cobra to reduce the snake population. Locals started breeding cobras to kill them and get paid. When the bounty stopped, they released the snakes.


"Perverse incentives create perverse outcomes."

Always play "Red Team" against your compensation plan. How would you cheat it?

Transparency & Communication

A compensation plan is a communication tool. If people don't understand it, it doesn't work.

Simplicity

If an employee cannot calculate their own bonus on a napkin, the plan is too complex.

Frequency

Provide feedback on progress towards the bonus monthly or quarterly, not just at year-end.

Realism

Targets must be "Stretch but Achievable". Impossible targets demotivate (Expectancy Theory).

Fairness & Equity

Employees compare themselves to others (Equity Theory).

Internal Equity

"Do I earn fair pay compared to my colleague Bob who does the same job?"

External Equity

"Do I earn fair pay compared to what I could get at Competitor X?"

Perceived unfairness leads to toxicity and turnover.

When Incentives Go Wrong

Wells Fargo (2016)

Incentive: Aggressive cross-selling targets (8 products per customer).

Outcome: Employees opened 3.5 million fake accounts to hit targets.


Enron (2001)

Incentive: Executives paid on stock price and revenue, not cash flow.

Outcome: Massive accounting fraud to inflate perceived value.

Modern HR looks at the whole picture.

Compensation
(Pay + Bonus)
Benefits
(Health, Pension)
Work-Life
(Flexibility)

To attract top talent, you need all five.

Conclusion

Compensation is not just an expense; it is a strategic lever.

Align incentives with your Strategy, make them fair, keep them simple, and watch out for unintended consequences.
