Business Strategy

The Art of Winning in Competitive Markets

What is Business Strategy?

Strategy is the direction and scope of an organization over the long term, which achieves advantage in a changing environment through its configuration of resources.


Michael Porter defines it as:

"Deliberately choosing a different set of activities to deliver a unique mix of value."

The Foundation: Vision, Mission, Values

Vision

Where are we going?
An aspirational future state. (e.g., "To accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy").

Mission

Why do we exist?
What the company does every day. (e.g., "To organize the world's information").

Values

How do we behave?
The guiding principles and ethics (e.g., Integrity, Innovation).

Three Levels of Strategy

  • 1. Corporate Strategy
    Decisions about which industries to compete in (M&A, Diversification).
  • 2. Business Strategy
    How to compete within a specific industry or market (Cost vs Differentiation).
  • 3. Functional Strategy
    Operational approaches for departments (Marketing, HR, R&D).

Industry Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

Determines the attractiveness and profitability of an industry.

  1. Threat of New Entrants: Barriers to entry.
  2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers: Supplier concentration.
  3. Bargaining Power of Buyers: Price sensitivity.
  4. Threat of Substitutes: Alternative solutions.
  5. Rivalry Among Competitors: Intensity of competition.

Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View (RBV)

VRIO Framework

To provide a sustained competitive advantage, a resource must be:

Valuable

Does it exploit opportunities or neutralize threats?

Rare

Is it controlled by only a few firms?

Inimitable

Is it costly for others to copy?

Organized

Is the firm ready to exploit it?

Competitive Advantage

Competitive Advantage occurs when a firm generates higher economic value than its competitors.


It usually comes from two sources:

  1. Cost Advantage: Producing similar products at lower cost.
  2. Differentiation Advantage: Producing unique products that command a premium price.

Strategy 1: Cost Leadership

How to achieve it?

  • Economies of Scale.
  • Proprietary Technology.
  • Preferential access to raw materials.

The Risk

Competitors may copy the technology, or customer preferences may shift towards quality over price.

Strategy 2: Differentiation

How to achieve it?

  • Superior Product Quality.
  • Branding and Marketing.
  • Superior Customer Service.

The Risk

The "premium" price becomes too high for the value perceived by the customer.

Strategy 3: Focus (Niche)

Serving a specific segment

The focuser selects a segment or group of segments in the industry and tailors its strategy to serving them to the exclusion of others.

Advantages

High customer loyalty, less competition, deep market knowledge.

Disadvantages

Limited growth potential, risk of the niche disappearing.

Blue Ocean Strategy

Stop competing. Create a new market space.


  • Red Ocean: Compete in existing market space. Beat the competition. Exploit existing demand.
  • Blue Ocean: Create uncontested market space. Make the competition irrelevant. Create and capture new demand.

Example: Cirque du Soleil
