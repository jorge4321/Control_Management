Operational Strategy

Designing the Engine of Business Success

What is Operational Strategy?

Operational Strategy is the total pattern of decisions which shape the long-term capabilities of any type of operation and their contribution to the overall strategy.


  • It is NOT just day-to-day management.
  • It aligns resources with market requirements.
  • It answers: "How do we execute our business model efficiently?"

The Strategic Hierarchy

Corporate Strategy
(What business are we in?)
↓
Business Strategy
(How do we compete?)
↓
Operational Strategy
(How do we execute?)

The 4 V's of Operations

Every operation differs in four key dimensions:

Volume: High volume usually allows for standardization and lower costs (e.g., McDonald's).
Variety: High variety implies flexibility and customization (e.g., A luxury tailor).
Variation: How much does demand change over time? High variation requires buffer capacity.
Visibility: How much of the process does the customer see? High visibility requires better staff skills.

The 5 Performance Objectives

Cost

Quality

Speed

Dependability

Flexibility

"You cannot be the best at everything. Strategy is about choosing."

1. Cost Strategy

Low Cost Operations

Focuses on producing goods or services at the lowest possible cost.


  • Economies of scale
  • Elimination of waste
  • Automation
  • High facility utilization

2. Quality Strategy

Doing Things Right

Providing error-free goods and services that fit the customer's purpose.


Impact: reduces rework costs, increases customer loyalty, and allows for premium pricing.

3. Speed Strategy

Doing Things Fast

Minimizing the time between the order and the delivery.

Internal Benefits

Reduces inventory risk and cash flow issues.

External Benefits

Provides instant gratification to customers (e.g., Amazon Prime).

4. Dependability Strategy

Doing Things On Time

Keeping promises. Delivering exactly when you said you would.


For a B2B company (like a car parts supplier), dependability is often more valuable than pure speed, as it allows clients to plan their own production (Just-In-Time).

5. Flexibility Strategy

Changing Things

The ability to adapt the operation.

Product Flexibility: Introducing new products easily.
Mix Flexibility: Changing the variety of products produced.
Volume Flexibility: Handling sudden spikes in demand.
Delivery Flexibility: Changing delivery dates.

Trade-offs & The Efficient Frontier

You cannot improve all objectives simultaneously. Improving one often degrades another.


High Variety

Higher Cost

High Speed

Lower Flexibility

Strategy is deciding which trade-off to accept.

Process Design & Layout

How resources are arranged determines efficiency.

  • Product Layout (Line): Tasks arranged in sequence (Assembly line). Fast, efficient, low flexibility.
  • Process Layout (Functional): Similar resources grouped together (Hospital depts). High flexibility, complex flow.
  • Cellular Layout: Self-contained teams working on families of products.

Capacity Planning

Matching supply with demand.

Level Strategy

Produce at a constant rate. Use inventory to absorb demand spikes.

Chase Strategy

Change production to match demand. Requires flexible labor.

Demand Management

Influence demand (pricing, promotions) to fit capacity.

Supply Chain Integration

Vertical Integration vs. Outsourcing

Vertical Integration: Owning the supply chain (Control, but high risk/cost).

Outsourcing: Buying from others (Flexibility, focus on core competencies).


Modern strategy focuses on Collaboration and Visibility across the entire chain.

Technology Strategy

Technology is not just IT; it's any hardware or software that processes material, information, or customers.

Industry 4.0

IoT, Big Data, and AI allowing for "Smart Factories".

Process Tech

Robotics, CNC machines, 3D Printing (Additive Manufacturing).

Lean Operations

Just-in-Time (JIT)

A philosophy that aims to minimize waste (Muda).

  • Produce only what is needed, when needed.
  • Continuous Improvement (Kaizen).
  • Respect for people.

Sustainable Operations

The "Triple Bottom Line": People, Planet, Profit.

Circular Economy

Designing products for reuse and recycling.

Energy Efficiency

Reducing carbon footprint in production.

Social Responsibility

Fair labor practices in the supply chain.

Operational Risk & Resilience

Building Resilience

How to recover from disruptions (Natural disasters, pandemics, supply failures).


Redundancy

Having backup suppliers or extra capacity (Costly but safe).

Agility

Ability to change production rapidly.

Alignment & Implementation

An operational strategy is useless if not implemented.

Top-Down: Operations must reflect what the business wants to be.

Bottom-Up: Operations learns from day-to-day experience and suggests new strategic possibilities (Emergent Strategy).

Conclusion

Operational Strategy is the art of aligning resources to create capabilities that give the company a competitive advantage.
